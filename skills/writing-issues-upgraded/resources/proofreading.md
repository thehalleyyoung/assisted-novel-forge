# Proofreading

Proofreading is the final surface pass after development, line editing, and
copyediting. It catches what slipped through or was introduced during
revision. It is not the place to rethink structure or voice unless a surface
error reveals a larger unresolved issue.

## Method

Read slowly and literally. Proofreading is the one editorial pass where you
are not reading for meaning, flow, or pleasure — you are reading for the
surface. Reading at normal speed causes your mind to auto-correct errors and
fill in missing words. Slow down.

Read each passage against the style sheet. Post-copyedit revisions
frequently introduce drift: a name that was standardized during copyedit
reverts to an earlier spelling, a capitalization convention is forgotten in
a new paragraph. Compare against the established decisions.

Separate your proofing pass from any developmental or line-level reactions.
If you notice a structural problem or a voice issue, note it under a
separate heading at the end of your report. Do not mix proofing corrections
with craft observations — the author may have closed the structural
revision and does not expect to reopen it.

## Check

- typos, missing words, duplicated words
- punctuation and capitalization slips
- spacing, indentation, paragraph breaks, chapter headings
- formatting consistency: scene breaks, section markers, layout artifacts
- wrong names, wrong homophones, accidental autocorrects
- style-sheet drift introduced after copyedit or during revision
- inconsistencies between the text and any front/back matter, table of
  contents, or cross-references

## Common AI-Specific Proofing Errors

These patterns appear more frequently in AI-generated or AI-revised text:

- Duplicated phrases where a revision was inserted without removing the
  original
- Orphaned references to deleted content (characters, locations, or events
  that were cut but still referenced)
- Tense or POV shifts at revision boundaries where new prose was inserted
  into existing text
- Inconsistent formatting between AI-generated and human-written sections
- Missing or doubled scene breaks at the edges of inserted passages

## Report

Keep the report terse and locatable. Each entry should give enough context
to find the error without re-reading the surrounding text: chapter, scene,
paragraph position, and a brief quote.

Group repeated patterns (e.g., “the manuscript consistently writes 'grey'
in chapters 1–4 and 'gray' in chapters 5–8”) rather than listing every
instance. List one-off errors individually.

If you notice developmental or line issues, put them under a separate
“beyond proofing” heading so the author can choose whether to reopen
the draft.
