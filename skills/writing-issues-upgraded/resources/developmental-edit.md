# Developmental Edit

Developmental editing addresses the book-level and scene-level architecture
before line polish. It asks whether the draft is the right shape for the
intended reader experience.

## Method

Read the full draft before diagnosing. A scene that looks weak in isolation
may be doing necessary setup; a scene that reads well may be structurally
redundant. You cannot make developmental judgments from a single passage.

Separate structural problems from execution problems. A scene that
dramatizes the right beat but does it clumsily is an execution issue — it
needs rewriting, not cutting. A scene that dramatizes the wrong beat, or a
beat that has no structural function, is a developmental issue no amount of
rewriting will fix.

Start with the largest unit you can see: does the overall arc deliver on its
premise? Then work down through act-level, chapter-level, and scene-level
structure. Problems at higher levels cascade: a broken act structure makes
chapter-level diagnosis unreliable.

## Check

- **Premise and promise**: what does the opening teach the reader to expect?
  Does the rest of the draft pay that promise, or does it quietly drift
  toward a different book?
- **Causality**: do events cause or complicate each other, or merely follow
  in sequence? Could scenes be reordered without breaking the story? If yes,
  the structure is a chronicle, not a plot.
- **Scene function**: what changes by the end of each scene — character
  knowledge, relationships, power dynamics, stakes, emotional state? If
  nothing changes, the scene is a candidate for cutting or compression.
- **Pacing**: where does pressure rise, plateau, rush, or sag? Watch for
  sustained intensity without variation (numbing) and sustained quiet
  without tension (drifting).
- **Character arc**: are decisions motivated by established want, fear,
  wound, pressure, or new information? Or do characters do what the plot
  needs regardless of who they are?
- **Stakes**: what can be lost now, and why does it matter to *this*
  character? Vague threats and stakes the reader can't feel don't create
  tension.
- **Information design**: what is withheld, revealed, repeated, or
  explained? Is the reader holding questions that create forward pull, or
  has the draft answered everything too early?
- **Genre contract**: is the draft paying the kind of reward it promised? A
  mystery that never lets the reader reason alongside the detective breaks
  its contract. A romance that doesn't let the reader feel the connection
  breaks its.

## Report

Prioritize fixes that change the reader's experience most. A developmental
note should usually point to a structural action: cut, move, expand, combine,
split, reframe, seed earlier, delay payoff, or change a character decision.
Name the action, explain the reader cost of the current version, and show
what the change would accomplish.

Do not solve developmental problems with line edits. If the scene lacks a
turn, prettier sentences will not fix it. If the arc sags in the middle,
polishing the prose in the middle will not give it momentum.
