# Copyedit

Copyediting protects correctness and consistency after the larger edit is
stable. It improves readability without changing author intent or voice.

## Method

Read linearly, not by jumping between sections. A copyedit catches errors
in the flow a reader will experience: inconsistencies in naming, tense
shifts that break mid-paragraph, timeline contradictions between adjacent
scenes. Jumping around misses these.

Build or update the style sheet as you go, not after. Every time you
encounter a new name, term, capitalization choice, or usage decision, log
it. The style sheet is the accumulating record of what "correct" means for
this project. Without it, you'll make inconsistent calls.

Distinguish between errors and choices. Non-standard grammar, sentence
fragments, run-ons, dialect spelling, and unconventional punctuation may be
the author's voice. If the manuscript uses them consistently, they are
choices. If they appear sporadically, they are likely errors. When you are
not sure, query rather than silently correct.

Fix silently when: the correction is unambiguous, preserves meaning and
voice, and falls within established style-sheet conventions. Query when: the
change could alter meaning, voice, tone, canon, or intentional dialect or
register. The rule is: if a reasonable reader could interpret the original
differently from the correction, query.

## Check

- spelling, grammar, usage, punctuation, capitalization
- tense, POV, number, pronoun reference, and modifier clarity
- repeated words, accidental echoes, and confusing sentence mechanics
- consistency of names, terms, hyphenation, numerals, timeline references
- dialogue punctuation and tag/action-beat mechanics
- style-sheet decisions and house/project style
- cross-references to canon, glossary, chapter names, or recurring terms

## Style Sheet

Track decisions rather than re-deciding them each time:

- character/place/organization names (spelling, capitalization, titles)
- invented terms and aliases (including how they are introduced)
- capitalization and hyphenation conventions
- punctuation conventions (serial comma, em dash usage, ellipsis style)
- spelling variants (honor/honour, gray/grey — pick one, be consistent)
- recurring grammar/usage choices that are part of voice (fragments,
  run-ons, non-standard constructions the author uses intentionally)

When a project has an existing style sheet, follow it. When it doesn't,
create one from the manuscript's existing patterns. Do not impose a house
style the author never agreed to.

## Report

Separate corrections, queries, and style-sheet updates. Corrections are
changes you made. Queries are changes that need the author's decision.
Style-sheet updates are new entries you added based on the manuscript's
patterns.

Group recurring errors as patterns rather than listing every instance. If
the manuscript consistently misuses a term or has a systematic punctuation
error, name the pattern once, give examples, and note the scope.
