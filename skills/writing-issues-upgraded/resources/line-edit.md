# Line Edit

Line editing improves how the prose moves while preserving the author's voice.
It operates after the scene or chapter is structurally sound enough to polish.

## Method

Read the passage at the pace a reader would, not scanning for errors. Line
editing is about felt prose quality: rhythm, momentum, clarity, and voice.
You are listening for where the prose stumbles, goes flat, over-explains, or
loses the character's way of seeing.

Learn the voice before you touch it. Read enough of the project to know what
"this writer's prose" sounds like — its characteristic rhythms, sentence
tendencies, vocabulary range, and emotional register. A line edit that
normalizes the author's voice into generic good prose has failed.

Name patterns, not every instance. A line edit that catalogs fifty individual
sentences teaches the author nothing about what's going wrong. Find the
three or four recurring patterns, name them, give representative examples,
and let the author apply the insight across the manuscript.

## Check

- **Voice**: does the language belong to this narrator, POV, character, genre,
  and project style? Or has the prose drifted toward competent-generic?
- **Rhythm**: do sentence lengths, paragraph breaks, punctuation, and beats
  vary with the emotional pressure? Or does the prose sit at one speed
  regardless of what's happening?
- **Clarity**: can the reader follow action, reference, geography, and thought
  without extra explanation? Confusion is not depth.
- **Texture**: are details specific and selected, or generic and piled on?
  One precise image does more than three adequate ones.
- **Subtext**: does dialogue carry more than surface information? Are
  characters saying what they mean, or is there a gap between the said and
  the meant?
- **Redundancy**: are emotions, motives, and implications stated after the
  text already made the reader infer them? This is the single most common
  line-level failure in AI-generated prose: the text does the work, then a
  sentence explains what the work accomplished.

## Report

For each pattern: name it, explain the reader cost, give two or three
representative examples with passage references, and describe the direction
of a fix. "Sharper POV," "cleaner image," "stronger rhythm," "less
explanation," "more pressure," "clearer referent" — say what the prose would
gain, not just what's wrong.

Preserve deliberate roughness, ambiguity, silence, repetition, or broken
rhythm when it serves the voice or scene effect. If you're unsure whether
something is a choice or a mistake, say so.
