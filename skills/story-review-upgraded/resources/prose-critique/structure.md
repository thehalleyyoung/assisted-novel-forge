# Structure Review

Evaluate plot logic, pacing, scene necessity, stakes, and setup/payoff.

## Plot Logic

Trace the causal chain. Each event should cause or be caused by other events: "and then" sequences (things that merely follow each other) indicate weak plot structure.

Questions to ask:
- Does this event follow logically from what came before?
- Are character decisions driving the plot, or is the plot happening to the characters?
- Are coincidences doing the work that character agency should?
- Could events be reordered without breaking the story? (If yes, the structure is a sequence, not a plot.)

## Pacing

Pacing problems are felt, not measured. Read the draft and notice where your attention drifts, where you skim, where you want more.

**Dragging**: scenes that run longer than their content warrants. Common causes: over-description, circular dialogue, repeated emotional beats, scenes that make one point in three pages.

**Rushing**: important moments that don't get enough space. Emotional beats that should land hard are summarized instead of dramatized. Character decisions that should feel weighty happen in a sentence.

**Monotone**: sustained intensity without variation, or sustained quiet without tension. Pacing needs rhythm: alternation between high and low energy.

**Scene-level pacing**: within a scene, does the tension build or does it plateau? Does the scene start where it should (not too early with setup) and end where it should (not lingering after the point is made)?

## Scene Necessity

Every scene should change something: character knowledge, relationships, power dynamics, stakes, emotional state. The test: what would be lost if this scene were cut?

Scenes that exist only to:
- Convey information (find a scene already doing something and put the info there)
- Show characters traveling or transitioning (cut to the destination)
- Recap previous events (trust the reader)
- Establish atmosphere without advancing anything (trim or cut)

...are candidates for cutting or combining.

## Stakes

What's at risk, and does the reader feel it? Stakes need to be clear, credible, and escalating.

**Clear**: the reader knows what could be lost. Vague threats ("everything could change") don't create tension.

**Credible**: the threat feels real. If the protagonist always wins, stakes are hollow. The story needs to demonstrate that failure is possible and sometimes deliver on that threat.

**Escalating**: later scenes/chapters should put more at risk than earlier ones. Stakes can escalate in kind (physical → emotional → moral), not just in magnitude.

## Setup and Payoff

Every significant payoff needs prior setup. Every significant setup needs eventual payoff.

**Missing setup**: a twist or revelation that has no groundwork. The reader should be able to look back and see it was built, even if they didn't notice at the time. Twists without setup feel like cheating.

**Abandoned setup**: foreshadowing, Chekhov's guns, or promises to the reader that never pay off. Readers track these consciously and unconsciously. Abandoned setup erodes trust.

**Too-obvious setup**: when the setup is so heavy-handed that the payoff is telegraphed. The ideal is setup that's invisible on first read and obvious on reread.
